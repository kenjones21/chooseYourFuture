Methodology

Choose Your Future

Carbon budgets in this document are based primarily on the budgets laid out in Table 2.2 of the AR5 Synthesis report. This table was used to create the divisions in the emissions pathway chart as well as the temperature corresponding to a 33% chance of exceedance. This was done by noting that the carbon budgets for various temperatures are linear (given the same probability of exceeding said temperature). This relationship may not hold at temperatures too far beyond 3 degrees Celsius.

However, it was not possible to use this table to inform probabilities of exceedance for intermediate levels of emissions. To create this, the AR5 scenario database was used. In the database, probabilities of exceedance are given for 1.5, 2, 3, and 4 degrees Celsius across model runs, using the reduced complexity MAGICC6 model. It is possible to compare cumulative carbon dioxide emissions to 2100 and probabilities of exceedance at the same date.

Initially, a smooth fit across the four temperatures was attempted, using LOWESS regression. However, because the data includes a wide range of scenarios at certain stabilization levels, the smoothing behavior tended to be strange (and arguably unphysical) in these areas. To rectify this, an assumption was made that the probability distribution function for the amount of cumulative emissions necessary to exceed a certain temperature threshold was normally distributed, so the data as given could be fit to the integral of a Gaussian. The two fits generally agree with one another pretty well. Perhaps the most important discrepancy is at low levels of exceedance of 4 degrees Celsius, where the Gaussian fit is somewhat larger than the LOWESS fit.

The stats for impact by degree were pulled from a report based on another report from the National Research Council. Beyond the regimes specified in the report, the stat is specified as unknown. The temperature used to generate the stats is the temperature corresponding to a 33% probability of exceedance. An alternative might have been to pick the median temperature, but the 33% temperature was chosen to be more representative of the damages one might expect, given that the second derivative of most damage functions with respect to temperature is positive for temperatures less than six degrees, and for some damage functions (DICE), every temperature. For instance, if one considers a damage function of the form given by Weitzman (2010) and a normally distributed PDF, the temperature representing the average damage is actually a little over a tenth of a degree higher than the mean temperature increase.

More qualitatively, it's arguably more important to communicate tail risks than the median case. The 33% chance of exceedance was chosen specifically because it is listed concretely in the AR5 Synthesis Report, and thus comes from a more authoritative source than the analysis done specifically for this interactive.

The 2030 scenarios come from Climate Action Tracker, corrected to go from CO2e to CO2, and Fawcett et. al. The general scenarios correspond to the RCP scenarios (2.6, 4.5, and 6 for low, medium, and high respectively), but only insofar as the budget to 2100 for carbon dioxide is the same; the emission pathway shown differs from the RCP scenarios, particularly for the medium and high scenarios. The committed general scenario corresponds to existing carbon dioxide commitments from existing infrastructure, as documented by Peter Erickson and Kevin Tempest. Historical data comes from the Global Carbon Project.
